This is a fascinating side effect of bash's parsing. 
eval() { echo "$_"; }; eval foo=(bar) is different from export() { echo "$_"; }; export foo=(bar); 

While correct, it's a tricky case to duplicate precisely, and one can easily quote the string. Fix later. 


#### <img src="https://avatars.githubusercontent.com/u/255938?v=4" width="50">[ormaaj](https://github.com/ormaaj) commented at [2014-07-17T18:03:31Z](https://github.com/koalaman/shellcheck/issues/25#issuecomment-49342099):

Just FYI, I'm reasonably sure this is a bash parser bug. `eval` is incorrectly classified as a declaration command. It occurs with several other builtins in situations that make no sense. I even mentioned it to Chet on a few occasions but he didn't comment. The arg should be quoted IMO. I at least documented this effect here long ago: 

http://wiki.bash-hackers.org/commands/builtin/eval#portability_considerations
