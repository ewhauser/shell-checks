#### Here's a snippet or screenshot that shows the problem:

```sh
#! /bin/bash

: ${BAR=a b c}
: ${FOO=$BAR}

echo "$FOO"
```

#### Here's what shellcheck currently says:
```
$ shellcheck /tmp/bar.sh

In /tmp/bar.sh line 3:
: ${BAR=a b c}
  ^-- SC2086: Double quote to prevent globbing and word splitting.


In /tmp/bar.sh line 4:
: ${FOO=$BAR}
  ^-- SC2086: Double quote to prevent globbing and word splitting.

```


#### Here's what I wanted or expected to see:

The use of `: ${v1=val} ${v2:=val}` to assign default values to variables is fairly common (e.g., the Autotools use this extensively), and I can't see how IFS splitting would matter in anyway here.  I agree this makes an exception and the less exceptions we are the happier we are, yet here, I think it would be worth it.

As far as I can tell, nothing's wrong with initializing variables a la `${v1=val}`, but the issue seems to be that it's reporting that `: ${BAR=a b c}` is equivalent to executing `: a b c`, that is `:` with three arguments, rather than the likely intended `: 'a b c'`. Given that `:` is a no-op command, it's not relevant in this example, but the warning is correct nonetheless: words would be split when being evaluated as-is.
