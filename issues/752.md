IMHO those extra braces are ugly, noisy, redundant, and cloud readability.
Your opinion is not universally shared.

Whether something is "easier" to read depends entirely on one's linguistic background. For many of us the braces -- when *not* performing a more complex expansion -- are annoying noise that we would prefer not to see.

For those of us who started programming in C or C++ or Java or Perl or Python, the boundaries of variable names are completely self-evident, so extra delimiters serve no benefit.

I'm reminded of the famous quote by Ralph Waldo Emerson *[consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/353571-a-foolish-consistency-is-the-hobgoblin-of-little-minds-adored)*.

For those of us accustomed to scripts without extra braces, the presence of an opening brace is a useful signal that a "complex expansion" follows, which needs to be read more carefully. A coding style that 'consistently' uses braces everywhere deprives us of that signal and makes it harder to pay extra attention to the complex expansions.

And then there's the tendency among newbies to believe that "brace quoting" `${foo}` is functionally equivalent to `"$foo"`, which leads them to write broken code when they leave out the quotes because they've 'already' quoted it.

-Martin

#### <img src="https://avatars.githubusercontent.com/u/4577396?v=4" width="50">[dirtyharrycallahan](https://github.com/dirtyharrycallahan) commented at [2023-09-06T13:43:48Z](https://github.com/koalaman/shellcheck/issues/752#issuecomment-1708399529):

I don't have an opinion but I will comment on the misused and misunderstood quote.

It is often even misquoted as simply "consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds" (or even more sloppy approximations like "conformity is a bugbear of small-minded people", etc.).

Here's the quotation in longer form, with more of its original context. It becomes immediately apparent that it has nothing to do with writing style, and everything to do with inflexible mentality:

A foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds, adored by little statesmen and philosophers and divines. With consistency a great soul has simply nothing to do. He may as well concern himself with his shadow on the wall. Speak what you think now in hard words, and to-morrow speak what to-morrow thinks in hard words again, though it contradict every thing you said to-day. — 'Ah, so you shall be sure to be misunderstood.' — Is it so bad, then, to be misunderstood? Pythagoras was misunderstood, and Socrates, and Jesus, and Luther, and Copernicus, and Galileo, and Newton, and every pure and wise spirit that ever took flesh. To be great is to be misunderstood.
— Ralph Waldo Emerson, "[Self-Reliance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-Reliance)", [Essays: First Series](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/16643/16643-h/16643-h.htm#SELF-RELIANCE), 1841

Most people who quote or misquote the famous part of this passage do so to criticize an argument for textual, stylistic, or other presentational consistency, and are usually doing so to advance some alternative style in a mentally inflexible way. In doing so, they're foolishly displaying an ironic ignorance of Emerson's actual meaning and intent, which was criticism of refusal to change one's mind or adjust one's position in light of new facts or different situations.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Emerson_and_Wilde_on_consistency

@srcshelton yes we can agree to disagree; we have diametrically opposed views on what is, at root, æsthetic preference born from our respective individual life experiences. Neither affects the correctness of a script.

@port19x I agree, ShellCheck should focus on correctness, and target issues that lead directly or indirectly to malfunctioning scripts. Æsthetic choices (like indentation or brace insertion/removal, or anything else that that can be automatically "repaired") more properly belong to shfmt.

Arguably the "VARCAP" rule is primarily an æsthetic choice, but it's in no-man's-land: shfmt cannot know whether the names of variables are significant outside the script itself, which leaves it to ShellCheck to offer advice on changing them.

---

@dirtyharrycallahan I'm aware that Emerson was critiquing mental inflexibility, so I'm twisting it a little - but not much.

I will criticize the use of "consistency" as the primary justification for a particular choice being "better", whenever that choice has made something else _less_ consistent (i.e. it's a disingenuous assertion), or whenever there's a consequent reduction in semantic redundancy (and thus impaired error correction).

This isn't about personal inflexibility on either side; I can and will write code however the house style dictates, even if I think it's ugly and harder to read, and I'm sure the proponents of such styles have similar flexibility. (There are a few very strange styles that I can barely read, much less write, but that's a self-sealing problem.)

---

Regarding Google, perhaps the most important thing in Google's house style for shell scripts is that anything longer than ~10 lines should not _be_ a shell script: it should be written in a different language. Their policy is to discourage writing new shell scripts, and to the extent that that's not possible, the aim of their Shell style guide is to make short scripts that read as if they were some other "more familiar" language.

This is a double-edged sword: anyone can easily read such a script, but they acquire (and keep) a flawed mental model of how the shell actually works, leading to the situation where, due to the author's unfamiliarity with the language, about 1% of _approved_ shell-related Piper commits _introduced_ bugs. Some significant portion of those were blind `s/plaintext/$variable/g` replacements, so in that context "always use braces" makes perfect sense.

Google's shell scripting guide does have a lot of good stuff in it, but it should not be taken wholesale as the basis for a general purpose guide intended to cover large or complex scripts; rather, such a guide should selectively choose items from Google's guide on an opt-in rather than opt-out basis.
