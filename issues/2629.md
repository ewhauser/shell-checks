#### Here's a snippet or screenshot that shows the problem:
```sh
#!/bin/sh
printf '\x41'
```

#### Here's what I wanted or expected to see:
~~~
In /tmp/example.sh line 2:
	printf '\x41'
	        ^--^ SC#### (warning): Use octal instead of hex escapes
	        ^--^ SC#### (warning): Replace 

Did you mean:
	printf '\101'
~~~

## Rationale
Character escape sequences of the form <code>\x<ins><var>HH</var></ins></code>, <code>\u<ins><var>HHHH</var></ins></code>, and <code>\U<ins><var>HHHHHHHH</var></ins></code> aren't portable in `printf` format strings:

~~~console
$ bash -c "printf '\\x41\n'"
A
$ dash -c "printf '\\x41\n'"
\x41
~~~

AFAIK, only octal escape sequences (<code>\\<ins><var>OOO</var></ins></code>) are sanctioned by POSIX in `printf` formats. Commands that recognise `printf`-like escapes (like `tr`) might be subject to similar portability hassles, although I haven't done too much research on this front.
